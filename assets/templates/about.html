Why Self Destructing Secrets?

Because even with the powers contained within RIPA our Government and the Police are attempting to bypass legal safeguards in order to decrypt your data!

The Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act contains within it a provision to punish people with upwards of 2 years in prision if they do not hand over decryption keys when the Police demand them. There are protections within the bill of which one is;

(3) For the purposes of this section a person shall be taken to have shown that he was not in possession of a key to protected information at a particular time if—

(a) sufficient evidence of that fact is adduced to raise an issue with respect to it; and

(b) the contrary is not proved beyond a reasonable doubt.

To this end Brass Horn Communications released the S53 project in mid 2015. This was a software/hardware combination which would destroy all data on a protected computer if it detected tampering (or if a panic button was triggered). This was a thought experiment for this defence but some have since argued that a more subtle approach may be needed as "trapdooring" your data or purposefully destroying it via a panic button could violate other laws.

Using RIPACrypt one could store the pass phrase (or a Bitlocker / LUKs keyfile etc) for a given computer in a Crypt. That computer could be configured to check-in every hour with a 3 hour expiry limit. If the computer was seized then RIPACrypt would destroy the data and timestamp when this was done. As the afflicted party you had no direct control over the destruction process.

THERE IS NO GUARANTEE THAT THE USE OF RIPACRYPT WILL PROTECT YOU FROM PROSECUTION
Failure to comply with a notice under the Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 carries a sentence of up to 5 years in prison.

Why Are You Doing This?

Many civil libertarians objected to the compelled decryption powers within RIPA but the past 12 months has seen a worrying rise in the Governments desire to weaken or attack encryption.

This came to a head in 2016 when the National Crime Agency attempted to bypass the protections within RIPA (having failed to enforce a s.49 Notice earlier) and force Lauri Love to provide the decryption keys to property he had requested be returned.

It has become apparant that between the NCA's disregard for the law and the Home Office's desire to push the Investigatory Powers Bill through we need someting to protect people from compelled decryption!
